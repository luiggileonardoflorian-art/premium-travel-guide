Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about refinancing and home equity borrowing.

What is the difference between a rate-and-term refinance and a cash-out refinance?
A rate-and-term refinance replaces your existing mortgage with a new one—typically to secure a lower interest rate, change from an adjustable to a fixed rate, or alter your loan term. Your balance stays roughly the same. A cash-out refinance also replaces your mortgage, but with a larger loan than you currently owe; the difference is paid to you as a lump sum, letting you access built-up equity. Because you're borrowing more, cash-out refinances usually carry slightly higher rates and require more equity in your home.
How do I evaluate whether refinancing makes financial sense?
A common starting point is break-even analysis: divide the total closing costs of the refinance by the monthly payment savings to find how many months it takes to recoup those costs. If you plan to stay in the home longer than that period, refinancing may make financial sense. Other factors include your remaining loan term, how significantly your rate would change, and any near-term life changes. Our refinance guides cover this in detail. Always confirm specific numbers with a licensed mortgage professional, as individual results vary.
What is the difference between a home equity loan and a HELOC?
A home equity loan delivers a lump sum at a fixed interest rate, repaid over a set term with predictable monthly payments. A Home Equity Line of Credit (HELOC) works more like a credit card secured by your home: you're approved for a maximum line and draw from it as needed during a draw period, paying interest only on the amount used. After the draw period, you repay what you borrowed—often at a variable rate. Home equity loans suit borrowers who want a defined payment for a specific purpose; HELOCs tend to work better for ongoing or uncertain expenses.
What factors influence the mortgage rate I'll be offered?
Mortgage rates are shaped by both macroeconomic and individual factors. On the macro side, lenders are influenced by bond market yields (especially the 10-year Treasury), inflation expectations, and broader credit conditions. On the individual side, your credit score, debt-to-income ratio, loan-to-value ratio, loan type, and loan term all affect the rate you're quoted. Two borrowers applying on the same day can receive meaningfully different rates depending on their financial profiles and the lenders they approach.
