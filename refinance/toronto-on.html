Why Toronto's Mortgage Market is Unique

Toronto is Canada's largest city and financial hub, with a housing market characterized by high demand, limited supply, and prices that are among the highest in the country. The Canadian mortgage system differs significantly from the U.S.—with shorter terms (typically 5 years), mandatory stress testing, and different down payment rules. Understanding these nuances is essential for anyone looking to buy or refinance in the Greater Toronto Area (GTA).

1. Current Market Dynamics

As of early 2026, the Toronto housing market is showing signs of stabilization after the rapid price increases of the early 2020s. The average price for a detached home in the city of Toronto is approximately $1.65 million, while in the broader 905 area code (suburbs), it's around $1.2 million. Condos remain a more affordable entry point, averaging $750,000‑$850,000 depending on location.

Mortgage rates in Canada are quoted differently than in the U.S. Most borrowers choose a 5‑year fixed or 5‑year variable rate, with terms typically ranging from 1 to 10 years. As of March 2026, 5‑year fixed rates are averaging 4.5%‑5.0% for insured mortgages (high‑ratio) and 5.0%‑5.5% for uninsured (conventional) loans. Variable rates are slightly lower but come with payment uncertainty.

2. Canadian Mortgage Basics

Understanding the Canadian system is key to navigating the Toronto market. Here are the fundamental differences from the U.S.:

  • Mortgage terms vs. amortization: Canadians typically renew their mortgage every 3‑5 years (the "term"), even though the total amortization may be 25 years. This means you'll shop for a new rate every few years.
  • Insured vs. conventional: If your down payment is less than 20%, you must purchase mortgage default insurance from CMHC, Sagen, or Canada Guaranty. This protects the lender, and the premium is added to your mortgage.
  • Stress test: All insured mortgages and many uninsured ones require you to qualify at a rate either 2% higher than your contract rate or the Bank of Canada's 5‑year benchmark rate (currently 5.25%), whichever is higher.
  • Prepayment privileges: Most Canadian mortgages allow you to make lump‑sum payments (typically 10‑20% of the original principal annually) without penalty, which can help pay down your mortgage faster.

3. Down Payment & First‑Time Buyer Programs

Canada's down payment rules are progressive:

  • 5% on the first $500,000 of the purchase price.
  • 10% on the portion between $500,000 and $1,000,000.
  • 20% minimum for homes over $1,000,000.

First‑time buyers in Ontario have access to several programs:

  • First-Time Home Buyer Incentive (FTHBI): A shared‑equity program with the government (now winding down but may still be available through some channels).
  • Home Buyers' Plan (HBP): Allows you to withdraw up to $35,000 from your RRSP tax‑free for a down payment, with 15 years to repay.
  • First-Time Home Buyers' Tax Credit: A $10,000 non‑refundable tax credit on your tax return.
  • Ontario Land Transfer Tax Refund: First‑time buyers in Ontario can receive a refund of up to $4,000 on the provincial land transfer tax. Toronto also levies its own land transfer tax, with a separate first‑time buyer rebate of up to $4,475.

4. Land Transfer Taxes & Closing Costs

Toronto is unique in Canada because it has a double land transfer tax—both the Province of Ontario and the City of Toronto levy the tax. The total rate is approximately:

  • 0.5%‑2.0% on the first $250,000 (combined).
  • 1.0%‑2.0% on the portion from $250,001 to $400,000.
  • 1.5%‑2.0% on the portion from $400,001 to $2,000,000.
  • 2.0%‑2.5% on amounts over $2,000,000.

For a $1M home, the total land transfer tax (provincial + Toronto) is approximately $33,475. First‑time buyers can get rebates up to $4,000 (provincial) and $4,475 (Toronto), significantly reducing this cost.

Other closing costs include legal fees ($1,000‑$2,500), title insurance (one‑time ~$500‑$1,000), home inspection ($500‑$800), and appraisal ($300‑$500). Always budget 1.5%‑4% of the purchase price for closing costs.

5. The Mortgage Stress Test Explained

The stress test is designed to ensure borrowers can still afford their mortgage if interest rates rise. Here's how it works:

You must qualify at the higher of:

  • Your contract rate + 2%, or
  • The Bank of Canada's 5‑year benchmark rate (currently 5.25%).

For example, if you get a 5‑year fixed rate of 4.5%, you must qualify at 6.5% (since 4.5%+2% = 6.5%, which is higher than 5.25%). This reduces your maximum borrowing amount. Many online calculators (including ours) allow you to input the stress test rate to see how it affects affordability.

“The stress test is the single biggest factor limiting borrowing power in Canada. Always calculate your qualification amount before you start house hunting.” — Luiggi V., Senior Mortgage Analyst

6. Refinance Strategies for Toronto Homeowners

Refinancing in Canada typically means breaking your current mortgage term and setting up a new one. This can involve prepayment penalties, so it's important to calculate the net benefit.

Rate‑and‑term refinance: If you're coming to the end of your term, you can simply renew with a new rate (often with your current lender, with no penalty). If you break a fixed‑rate mortgage early, you'll typically pay the greater of 3 months' interest or the interest rate differential (IRD)—which can be substantial.

Cash‑out refinance (refinance to access equity): You can increase your mortgage amount to access home equity, but you'll be subject to the stress test again. Maximum loan‑to‑value is typically 80% for a refinance (or 65% if you want to avoid insurance).

Always consult a mortgage broker to compare the cost of breaking your mortgage against the potential savings from a lower rate.

7. Local Lenders & Where to Start

Toronto has a robust lending market, including the Big Six banks, credit unions, and mono‑line lenders. For educational purposes, here are some lender types to consider (we do not endorse any specific institution):

  • Big Six banks: RBC, TD, Scotiabank, BMO, CIBC, and National Bank offer a full range of products and often have special rates for existing clients.
  • Credit unions: Meridian, DUCA, and Alterna Savings may offer competitive rates and flexible underwriting.
  • Mono‑line lenders: Companies like MCAP, First National, and Street Capital are mortgage specialists and often have low rates.
  • Mortgage brokers: In Canada, brokers are widely used and can shop multiple lenders for you. Look for brokers licensed in Ontario and with experience in the Toronto market.

Before applying, gather your documentation: Notice of Assessment (NOA) from CRA, T4s (2 years), pay stubs, bank statements, and if self‑employed, financial statements. For first‑time buyers, also have your RRSP statements if using the Home Buyers' Plan.

Frequently Asked Questions (Toronto)

What is the minimum down payment in Toronto?

In Canada, the minimum down payment is 5% for the first $500,000 of the purchase price, and 10% for the portion between $500,000 and $1 million. For homes over $1 million, the minimum down payment is 20%.

How does the mortgage stress test work in Canada?

The stress test requires borrowers to qualify at a rate either 2% higher than their contract rate or the Bank of Canada's 5‑year benchmark rate, whichever is higher. This ensures you could still afford payments if rates rise.

What are typical closing costs in Toronto?

Closing costs in Toronto typically range from 1.5% to 4% of the purchase price. They include land transfer taxes (Ontario and Toronto both levy these), legal fees, title insurance, and disbursements. First‑time buyers may qualify for rebates up to $4,000 (provincial) and $4,475 (Toronto).

Can I get a 30‑year amortization in Canada?

Yes, but only for uninsured mortgages (down payment ≥20%). Insured mortgages (high‑ratio) are limited to 25 years. A 30‑year amortization lowers your monthly payment but increases total interest paid.
