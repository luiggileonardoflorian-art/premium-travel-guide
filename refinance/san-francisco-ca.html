Why San Francisco’s Mortgage Market Operates Differently

The concentration of equity‑compensated households, the structural tax constraints of Proposition 13, and the prevalence of multi‑generational wealth make San Francisco a outlier market. Borrowers must navigate lender RSU policies, assess the long‑term tax benefit of low basis property, and often structure loans around concentrated stock positions.

RSU as Collateral — Not Just Income

In SF, RSUs are often the primary wealth vehicle. Conventional underwriting treats vested RSUs as income, but private banks and specialty lenders go further: pledged‑asset mortgages (PAMs) allow borrowers to collateralize concentrated stock positions, creating liquidity for a down payment without triggering capital gains. For Meta or Nvidia executives with millions in unvested RSUs, certain lenders now offer “RSU forward” structures that consider future vests in qualifying.

Proposition 13 & Intergenerational Transfer

Prop 13 caps property tax increases to 2% annually, based on a 1976 assessment base. A home purchased decades ago may have a tax bill 80% below market rate. When financing such properties, lenders must understand the retained low basis — refinancing or cash‑out can trigger reassessment under Prop 13 exclusions. For heirs, the parent‑child transfer exclusion preserves the low base. This creates unique lending scenarios where keeping old financing may be superior to a new loan.

Foreign National & Venture Capital Liquidity

San Francisco attracts substantial foreign capital — Chinese, Canadian, and European buyers often purchase luxury condos with cash or through foreign national loan programs. Meanwhile, venture capital partners with carried interest face unique income‑documentation challenges; asset‑based lending and “bank statement” programs are common. The city’s lender ecosystem includes specialists who underwrite GP commitments and distribution waterfall projections.

“In San Francisco, the typical borrower has more stock than salary. The lenders who win are those willing to underwrite a balance sheet, not just a W‑2.”

SF Luxury Enclaves & Financing Nuances

  • Pacific Heights / Presidio Heights: $5M–$30M Victorian/Edwardian; super‑jumbo ARMs, portfolio lenders only; many properties are historic — additional underwriting for renovation escrows.
  • Noe Valley / Eureka Valley: $2.5M–$5M family homes; strong demand from tech execs with children; jumbo conforming up to $2M, above that requires RSU‑friendly underwriting.
  • Russian Hill / North Beach: $2M–$8M; steep slopes require additional geotech reports; condo financing stricter — warrantable vs. non‑warrantable buildings.
  • Marin County (Belvedere, Tiburon): $3M–$15M water view estates; often second homes — vacation home jumbo rates and DSCR considerations for rented guest houses.

Proposition 13 & Financing

Prop 13 caps annual property tax increases to 2% of the 1976 base (or acquisition value). For long‑held homes, this creates massive tax advantages. Refinancing does NOT trigger reassessment, but cash‑out refinances may be limited by lender LTV rules. Transferring property between parents and children retains the low basis if a reassessment exclusion is claimed — crucial for estate planning.

Strategic insight: When purchasing a home in SF, your property tax basis resets to purchase price (approx 1.2% of sale). But if you inherit a family home with low Prop 13 basis, you may keep that low tax bill — lenders can underwrite the actual (low) taxes rather than market‑rate projections.

High Tax, High Service Trade‑off

California’s 13.3% top marginal rate is the nation’s highest. For a $2M annual income, the state tax burden is ~$266k. However, for mortgage qualifying, lenders use after‑tax income; the high state tax reduces DTI capacity. Some lenders gross‑up for state tax, but only if properly documented.

SF Frequently Asked Questions

How do I qualify using RSUs if I work at a public tech company?
Lenders will average 24 months of RSU vesting (Schedule D, W‑2). You need a two‑year history and a letter from employer confirming future grants. Stock price volatility can be mitigated by using a lower 12‑month average if the trend is upward. Private banks may also consider unvested RSUs with a discount.
Can I get a mortgage if my income is mainly carried interest / capital gains?
Yes — via asset depletion or bank statement programs. Some lenders accept K‑1 income, but carried interest is often unpredictable. A pledged asset mortgage using your investment account is the most straightforward path.
Does Prop 13 affect my refinance or cash‑out?
No, refinancing does not trigger reassessment. However, a cash‑out refi may be limited by the 80% LTV rule (for homestead). Also, if you add a new owner (e.g., spouse after marriage), that can trigger reassessment. Always consult a property tax attorney.
