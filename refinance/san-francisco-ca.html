Why San Francisco's Mortgage Market is Unique

San Francisco sits at the epicenter of the global technology industry, creating a mortgage market driven by high incomes, significant wealth concentration, and complex compensation structures. Stock options, RSUs, and pre-IPO planning are as central to the mortgage process as credit scores and down payments. Combined with California's high property values and tax rates, borrowers face a uniquely challenging—and rewarding—financing environment.

1. Current Market Dynamics

As of early 2026, the San Francisco housing market remains one of the most expensive in the country. The median home price in San Francisco County hovers around $1.6M, while in sought-after neighborhoods like Pacific Heights, Noe Valley, and the Marina, prices frequently exceed $3M. Across the bay in Silicon Valley (Palo Alto, Menlo Park), prices are even higher.

Mortgage rates for a 30‑year fixed conventional loan are currently averaging 6.5%‑6.9% for well‑qualified borrowers with 20% down. However, because most loans in the Bay Area are jumbo (above $806,500), the jumbo market is where the real action happens. Jumbo 30‑year fixed rates are highly competitive, often ranging from 6.25% to 6.5% for borrowers with strong credit and significant assets.

2. Using Stock Options & RSUs to Qualify

For many Bay Area tech employees, a significant portion of compensation comes in the form of stock options, restricted stock units (RSUs), or employee stock purchase plans (ESPPs). Traditional mortgage underwriting, which focuses on base salary and bonus, often doesn't capture this full picture. Fortunately, many lenders now offer programs that incorporate equity compensation.

RSUs: Lenders typically average the last 2 years of vested RSU income, as shown on tax returns (if you sold) or pay stubs. If you have a history of selling RSUs and the income is consistent, it can be used just like bonus income.

Stock options (NSOs/ISOs): These are more complex. Lenders will look at historical exercise patterns and may require a CPA letter to verify income from exercised options. For pre-IPO companies, some private banks offer asset-based loans that consider the value of your vested options, even if they aren't yet liquid.

ESPP: Discounted stock purchase plans can be used to show additional income, especially if you have a history of selling the shares immediately.

Tip: Work with a lender who has a dedicated "private client" or "tech lending" desk. They'll understand how to structure your file to maximize your qualifying income.

3. Pre‑IPO & Asset‑Based Lending

San Francisco is home to hundreds of private companies on the path to IPO. Employees of these companies often hold significant paper wealth but have limited liquid income. This is where asset‑based lending shines.

Asset depletion loans: Some lenders will calculate a monthly "income" based on your liquid assets (cash, stocks, bonds). For example, if you have $1M in assets, they might count 1% of that ($10,000/month) as income to help you qualify.

Securities‑backed lines of credit: Private banks may offer loans secured by your investment portfolio. These aren't traditional mortgages, but they can provide liquidity for a home purchase while you wait for an IPO or liquidity event. However, they often have variable rates and require you to maintain a certain portfolio value.

QSBS (Qualified Small Business Stock): For founders and early employees, gains from selling QSBS may be partially or fully exempt from federal tax. This can significantly increase after‑tax proceeds available for a down payment. Consult a tax advisor to understand your QSBS status.

4. Jumbo Loans & Private Banking

Given the prevalence of jumbo loans, the Bay Area has a highly competitive private banking landscape. Institutions like J.P. Morgan Private Bank, First Republic (now part of JPMorgan Chase), Citi Private Bank, and Bank of America Private Bank all have strong local presence.

Private banks often offer:

  • Interest‑only periods (typically 5‑10 years) to lower initial payments.
  • Flexible underwriting that considers total assets, not just income.
  • Relationship pricing if you move investment accounts to the bank.
  • Pre‑approval letters that carry weight with sellers and listing agents.

However, these relationships may require moving significant assets (often $1M+), so it's important to weigh the benefits against any costs or restrictions.

5. Condos, TICs, and Co‑ownership

In San Francisco, condos are a popular entry point, but many buildings have specific financing requirements. Lenders will check if the condo project is warrantable (meets Fannie Mae/Freddie Mac guidelines). If a building has too many investor units, pending litigation, or deferred maintenance, it may be non‑warrantable, requiring a larger down payment and higher rate.

TICs (Tenancy‑in‑Common): San Francisco has many TIC buildings, especially in areas where condominium conversion is restricted. TIC financing is more complex—fewer lenders offer it, and down payment requirements are often higher (25‑30%). If you're buying a TIC, work with a lender experienced in this niche.

Co‑ownership loans: Some lenders now offer products specifically for groups of buyers (friends, family) purchasing a home together. These loans consider the combined income and assets of all borrowers and structure ownership accordingly.

6. California Taxes & SALT Considerations

California's top marginal income tax rate of 13.3% is the highest in the nation. Combined with the federal SALT deduction cap of $10,000, high‑income Bay Area residents face a significant tax burden that affects overall affordability.

Property taxes in California are limited by Proposition 13, which caps the base tax rate at 1% of the purchase price and limits annual increases to 2%. However, when you buy, your property taxes are reassessed based on the purchase price, so on a $2M home, you're looking at roughly $20,000/year in property taxes.

Transfer taxes: San Francisco imposes a steep real estate transfer tax—currently 0.8% for properties over $5M, and higher for properties over $10M and $25M. This can add tens of thousands to your closing costs.

When budgeting, always include property taxes and transfer taxes in your cash‑to‑close calculations. Your lender will also escrow for property taxes, increasing your monthly payment.

7. Refinance Strategies for Bay Area Homeowners

With rates higher than the historic lows of 2020‑2021, refinancing may not make sense for everyone. However, if you have an ARM that is about to reset, or if you bought with a rate above 7%, it's worth exploring current jumbo rates.

Cash‑out refinancing is popular in the Bay Area for accessing home equity to fund renovations, diversify investments, or help family members with down payments. Given the rapid appreciation, many homeowners have substantial equity. Be aware that cash‑out loans typically have slightly higher rates than rate‑and‑term refinances.

For those with significant stock holdings, consider a securities‑backed line of credit instead of a cash‑out refinance—it may offer more flexibility and avoid resetting your mortgage rate.

8. Local Lenders & Where to Start

The Bay Area has a rich ecosystem of lenders specializing in tech wealth. For educational purposes, here are some lender types to consider (we do not endorse any specific institution):

  • Private banks: J.P. Morgan Private Bank, First Republic (now part of JPMorgan Chase), Citi Private Bank, Bank of America Private Bank.
  • Local credit unions: San Francisco Fire Credit Union, Patelco Credit Union—often offer competitive rates for conforming loans.
  • Mortgage brokers specializing in tech: Firms like Equity Prime, OwnUp, or local brokers who understand stock options and RSUs.
  • Portfolio lenders: Some local banks hold loans in their portfolio, allowing for more flexible underwriting.

Before applying, gather your documentation: tax returns (2 years), recent pay stubs, bank statements, stock option/RSU grant letters, and vesting schedules. For self‑employed borrowers, also prepare profit‑loss statements.

Frequently Asked Questions (San Francisco)

What is the jumbo loan limit in San Francisco for 2026?

In San Francisco County, the conforming loan limit for 2026 is $806,500 for a single‑family home. Loans above this amount are considered jumbo and typically require larger down payments (20‑30%) and stronger credit scores. Given the high cost of Bay Area real estate, jumbo loans are the norm.

How can I use stock options to qualify for a mortgage?

Many lenders in San Francisco offer programs that consider vested stock options and RSUs as qualifying income. You'll need to provide documentation of the options (vesting schedules, exercise prices) and typically need a CPA to verify historical income from exercised options. Some private banks also offer asset‑based loans that use your total portfolio value to qualify.

What are typical closing costs in San Francisco?

Closing costs in San Francisco typically range from 2% to 4% of the loan amount, including lender fees, title insurance, escrow fees, and prepaid items. California also imposes a documentary transfer tax at the county and city level—in San Francisco, the combined rate can be as high as 0.8% of the purchase price for properties over certain thresholds.

What is a TIC and can I get a mortgage for one?

A TIC (Tenancy‑in‑Common) is a form of co‑ownership common in San Francisco where multiple owners hold fractional interests in a property. TIC financing is available but more limited—fewer lenders offer it, and down payment requirements are often higher (25‑30%). Work with a lender experienced in TIC transactions.
