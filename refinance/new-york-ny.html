Why Miami's Mortgage Market is Unique

Miami has transformed into a global financial hub, often called "Wall Street South." This influx of domestic and international wealth has created a unique mortgage landscape characterized by high jumbo loan volume, foreign national financing programs, and a significant market for luxury waterfront properties. Florida's absence of state income tax adds another layer of appeal for high‑net‑worth buyers.

1. Current Market Dynamics

As of early 2026, Miami's housing market remains robust, driven by continued migration from high‑tax states and international buyers seeking a stable U.S. real estate investment. Median home prices in Miami‑Dade County are approximately $650,000, with luxury enclaves like Star Island, Fisher Island, and Coconut Grove frequently seeing sales above $5 million.

Mortgage rates for a 30‑year fixed conventional loan are currently averaging 6.5%‑6.9% for well‑qualified borrowers with 20% down. Jumbo loans—those above the conforming limit of $806,500—are highly competitive, with private banks and local lenders offering rates as low as 6.25% for borrowers with significant assets.

2. Foreign National Financing

Miami is one of the top U.S. destinations for international homebuyers, particularly from Latin America and Europe. Many of these buyers do not have a U.S. credit history, which has led to a specialized niche: foreign national loan programs.

These loans typically require:

  • Higher down payments (30‑50% is common).
  • Documentation of international income (often requiring translation and certification).
  • Larger reserve requirements (6‑12 months of payments in a U.S. bank account).
  • An Individual Taxpayer Identification Number (ITIN) instead of a Social Security number.

Interest rates for foreign national loans are typically 0.5%‑1.5% higher than conventional loans, reflecting the additional risk. However, some private banks offer competitive rates for high‑net‑worth clients who move significant assets to the institution.

It's essential to work with a lender experienced in international transactions, as they understand the nuances of foreign income verification and can guide you through the ITIN application process.

3. Asset‑Based Lending & Private Banking

Miami's concentration of wealth has attracted numerous private banks and wealth management firms that offer asset‑based lending. Instead of focusing solely on W‑2 income, these lenders consider your total liquid assets—securities, cash, and sometimes even art or other collectibles—to qualify you for a mortgage.

This approach is particularly beneficial for:

  • Entrepreneurs with variable income.
  • Wealthy individuals with significant investment portfolios but modest salary.
  • Retirees living off investment income.

Private banks may also offer interest‑only periods, flexible prepayment terms, and the ability to pledge assets as additional collateral. However, these relationships often require moving substantial assets to the bank, so it's important to evaluate the overall cost and benefits.

4. Waterfront Properties & Condo Financing

Miami's iconic waterfront properties come with their own financing considerations. Lenders may require additional documentation for properties in flood zones, including proof of flood insurance and, in some cases, an elevation certificate to determine risk.

Condo financing in Miami also has unique aspects. Many luxury high‑rises have a high percentage of investor‑owned units or foreign owners, which can affect loan eligibility. Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac have specific guidelines for condos: no single entity can own more than 10% of the units, and at least 50% of the units must be owner‑occupied or second homes. If a building doesn't meet these criteria, you may need a non‑warrantable condo loan, which typically carries a higher rate and down payment requirement.

Before making an offer on a condo, ask your lender to check if the building is Fannie Mae approved. If not, you'll need to budget for a non‑warrantable loan.

5. Florida Tax Advantages

Florida's lack of state income tax is a major draw for high‑income earners and retirees. This can significantly improve affordability compared to high‑tax states like New York or California.

Additionally, Florida has a homestead exemption that reduces property taxes for primary residences. The exemption automatically applies to the first $25,000 of assessed value for all homeowners, and an additional $25,000 exemption for values between $50,000 and $75,000. There are also exemptions for seniors, disabled persons, and veterans.

However, Florida does impose a documentary stamp tax on mortgages (0.35% of the loan amount) and on deeds (0.7% of the purchase price). These are typically paid at closing and should be factored into your cash‑to‑close estimate.

6. Refinance Strategies for Miami Homeowners

With rates having risen from historic lows, refinancing may not be beneficial for everyone. However, if you have an adjustable‑rate mortgage that is about to reset, or if you purchased with a rate above 7%, it's worth exploring current jumbo rates.

Cash‑out refinancing is popular in Miami for accessing home equity to renovate, purchase additional property, or diversify investments. Given Miami's rapid appreciation, many homeowners have substantial equity. Be aware that cash‑out loans typically have slightly higher rates than rate‑and‑term refinances, and Florida law has specific protections for homestead properties regarding equity extraction.

7. Local Lenders & Where to Start

Miami has a diverse lending ecosystem. For educational purposes, here are some lender types to consider (we do not endorse any specific institution):

  • International private banks: Citi Private Bank, J.P. Morgan, and HSBC have strong international desks and foreign national programs.
  • Local community banks: City National Bank of Florida and Gibraltar Private Bank often have flexible underwriting for high‑net‑worth clients.
  • Credit unions: Dade County Federal Credit Union may offer competitive rates for conforming loans.
  • Mortgage brokers specializing in foreign national loans: They can shop multiple lenders to find the best terms for your specific situation.

Before applying, gather your documentation. For U.S. citizens: tax returns, pay stubs, bank statements. For foreign nationals: certified international income documentation, bank statements, ITIN (if available), and proof of funds for down payment and reserves.

Frequently Asked Questions (Miami)

What is the jumbo loan limit in Miami for 2026?

In Miami‑Dade County, the conforming loan limit for 2026 is $806,500 for a single‑family home. Loans above this amount are considered jumbo and typically require larger down payments (20‑30%) and stronger credit scores.

How does foreign national financing work in Miami?

Many Miami lenders offer programs for foreign nationals without U.S. credit history. These typically require a larger down payment (30‑50%), proof of international income, and higher reserves. Interest rates may be slightly higher than conventional loans.

What are typical closing costs in Miami?

Closing costs in Miami typically range from 2% to 4% of the loan amount, including lender fees, title insurance, surveys, and prepaid items. Florida also has a documentary stamp tax on mortgages (0.35% of the loan amount).

Can I finance a condo in Miami if the building has many investors?

If a condo building doesn't meet Fannie Mae guidelines (e.g., too many investor units), it's considered non‑warrantable. You may still get a loan, but it will likely require a larger down payment and carry a higher interest rate. Always check with your lender before making an offer.
