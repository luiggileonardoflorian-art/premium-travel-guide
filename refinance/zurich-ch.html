Why Zurich's Mortgage Market is Unique

Zurich is Switzerland's largest city and its undisputed financial capital, home to global banks, insurance giants, and a thriving tech scene. This concentration of wealth creates a unique housing market characterized by high prices, low availability, and a sophisticated mortgage landscape. The Swiss mortgage system differs significantly from other countries—with its two-mortgage structure, integration of pension funds (pillar 3a), and the unique SARON variable-rate products. Understanding these nuances is essential for anyone looking to buy or refinance in Zurich.

1. Current Market Dynamics

As of early 2026, the Zurich housing market remains one of the most expensive in Europe, driven by strong demand, limited supply, and the city's status as a safe haven for international capital. The average price for an apartment in the city of Zurich is approximately CHF 1.4 million, with significant variation by neighbourhood. Prime areas like Zurichberg, Enge, and Seefeld command premiums (CHF 2‑5 million+), while more affordable options exist in districts like Aussersihl and Hard (CHF 800,000‑1.2 million).

Swiss mortgage rates are among the lowest in the world, reflecting the country's stable economy and safe‑haven status. As of March 2026, 10‑year fixed mortgages average 1.6%‑2.0%, while 5‑year fixed rates are slightly lower at 1.4%‑1.8%. SARON (variable) mortgages, tied to the Swiss Average Rate Overnight, are available at around SARON +0.6%‑1.0% margin, currently resulting in all‑in rates of 1.2%‑1.6%.

2. Swiss Mortgage Basics

The Swiss mortgage system has several unique features that borrowers must understand:

  • Two‑mortgage structure: Swiss mortgages are typically split into two parts. The first mortgage covers up to 65% of the property value and requires no amortization. The second mortgage covers the next 15% (up to 80% total LTV) and must be amortized within 15 years or by retirement age. Any amount above 80% requires additional equity.
  • Interest‑only payments: Most Swiss mortgages are interest‑only, meaning you only pay interest on the loan. Amortization is handled separately for the second mortgage, typically through regular payments or a linked savings/pension product.
  • Equity requirement: You must provide at least 20% equity (cash or pension assets). At least half of this (10% of property value) must be "hard" cash—not from pension funds.
  • Affordability calculation: Lenders use a hypothetical interest rate of around 5% to calculate whether you can afford the mortgage, plus 1% for amortization and 1% for maintenance. This stress test ensures you can handle rate increases.

3. SARON vs. Fixed‑Rate Mortgages

Swiss borrowers can choose between fixed‑rate mortgages and variable‑rate products tied to SARON:

Fixed‑rate mortgages lock in your interest rate for a set period (typically 2‑15 years). They offer payment certainty and are the most popular choice. Rates are determined by the swap market and your lender's margin.

SARON mortgages are variable‑rate products where the interest rate adjusts quarterly based on the Swiss Average Rate Overnight (SARON), plus a lender margin. They typically offer lower initial rates than fixed mortgages but carry the risk of rate increases. SARON has replaced LIBOR as the Swiss reference rate.

Many borrowers choose a mix—for example, fixing part of the mortgage for security and using SARON for the remainder to lower average cost.

4. Using Pillar 3a Pension for Your Mortgage

One of the most distinctive features of Swiss mortgages is the ability to integrate your pillar 3a retirement savings. There are two main strategies:

  • Pledging (Verpfändung): You can pledge your pillar 3a account as collateral for the mortgage. This counts toward your equity requirement without actually withdrawing the funds. You continue to earn interest on the pension savings, and the pledge is released once you've built sufficient equity.
  • Early withdrawal (Vorbezug): You can withdraw funds from pillar 3a to use as equity for a primary residence. Withdrawn amounts are taxed as income but at a reduced rate. You cannot withdraw for a second home or investment property.

Many first‑time buyers use a combination of cash and pledged pillar 3a to reach the 20% equity threshold. Consult a tax advisor to understand the implications of early withdrawal.

“Pillar 3a is often called the 'secret weapon' for Swiss homebuyers. Pledging allows you to use your retirement savings without losing tax advantages or investment growth.” — Luiggi V., Senior Mortgage Analyst

5. Purchase Costs & Taxes

When buying a home in Zurich, you'll need to budget for several upfront costs beyond the purchase price:

  • Property transfer tax (Handänderungssteuer): In Zurich canton, this is approximately 1‑2% of the purchase price, depending on the municipality. Some cantons have no transfer tax.
  • Notary and land register fees: Typically 0.5‑1% of the purchase price for drafting contracts and registering ownership.
  • Appraisal fee (Schätzung): CHF 1,000‑3,000, required by the lender to confirm the property's value.
  • Mortgage setup fee (Bearbeitungsgebühr): Usually 0.5‑1% of the loan amount, though some lenders waive this for larger loans.
  • Legal fees (if using a lawyer): Optional but recommended for complex purchases.

Total upfront costs typically range from 3% to 5% of the purchase price. These cannot be included in your mortgage—you must pay them from your equity funds.

Property taxes: In Switzerland, property taxes are low and vary by canton and municipality. In Zurich, you'll pay an annual property tax (Liegenschaftssteuer) of about 0.3‑0.5‰ of the property value, plus income tax on the imputed rental value (Eigenmietwert)—a unique Swiss concept where you pay tax on the "rental income" you would receive if you rented the property.

6. Refinance Strategies for Zurich Homeowners

Refinancing in Switzerland typically means either extending your fixed‑rate period at maturity or switching lenders to secure better terms. Key considerations:

  • At maturity: When your fixed‑rate period ends, you can negotiate a new rate with your current lender or shop around. Switching lenders may involve new appraisal and legal fees (typically 0.5‑1% of the loan), so calculate the break‑even point.
  • Early termination: If you want to break a fixed mortgage before maturity, you'll pay a penalty equal to the interest rate difference over the remaining term. This can be substantial in a falling rate environment.
  • Releasing equity (Wertvermehrung): If your property has increased in value, you may be able to increase your mortgage to access equity. This is subject to the same 80% LTV limit and affordability test.
  • Switching from SARON to fixed: If you have a SARON mortgage and want rate certainty, you can switch to a fixed product, usually without penalty as variable rates have no fixed term.

Always consult a mortgage broker (Hypothekarvermittler) to compare offers from multiple lenders—they can access rates not publicly advertised.

7. Expat & International Buyer Financing

Zurich's international workforce means many lenders offer mortgages to expats and cross‑border commuters. Key points:

  • Residence permit: Most lenders require a valid B or C permit (or Swiss citizenship). Cross‑border commuters (Grenzgänger) from Germany, France, Italy, and Austria can also qualify with appropriate documentation.
  • Income verification: You'll need employment contracts, recent pay slips, and tax assessments. Income in foreign currencies may be discounted by 10‑20% to account for exchange rate risk.
  • Equity requirements: Same as for Swiss citizens—20% minimum, with at least half as "hard" cash.
  • Lex Koller: Non‑EU/EFTA citizens face restrictions on purchasing property in Switzerland. They generally need a residence permit and may require cantonal approval. EU/EFTA citizens have fewer restrictions but should verify their status.

Work with a mortgage advisor experienced in expat financing—they know which lenders are most flexible with international income and residency situations.

8. Local Lenders & Where to Start

Zurich has a competitive lending market including the big cantonal banks, national players, and specialist lenders. For educational purposes, here are some lender types to consider (we do not endorse any specific institution):

  • Cantonal banks (Kantonalbanken): Zürcher Kantonalbank (ZKB) is the largest and offers competitive rates for residents. Other cantonal banks may lend outside their canton.
  • Big banks: UBS and Credit Suisse (now part of UBS) offer a full range of mortgage products and have dedicated expat desks.
  • Raiffeisen: A cooperative bank with strong local presence and competitive rates, especially for members.
  • Mortgage brokers (Hypothekarvermittler): Companies like Moneypark, Hypotheke.ch, and Comparis can shop multiple lenders and find the best terms. They're paid by the lender, so their service is free to you.

Before applying, gather your documentation: employment contract, recent pay slips, tax assessments (Steuerveranlagung), bank statements, pillar 3a statements (if pledging), and ID/passport. For expats, also provide residence permit and proof of 30% ruling if applicable.

Frequently Asked Questions (Zurich)

What is the maximum mortgage I can get in Zurich?

In Switzerland, you typically need at least 20% equity to purchase a home. This means the maximum loan-to-value (LTV) is 80% of the property's value. For the first 65% (first mortgage), there are no amortization requirements. The remaining 15% (second mortgage) must be amortized within 15 years or by retirement age.

What are SARON mortgages?

SARON (Swiss Average Rate Overnight) mortgages are variable-rate products tied to the Swiss reference rate. They typically offer lower initial rates than fixed mortgages but carry the risk of rate increases. SARON rates adjust quarterly and are popular in the current low-rate environment.

Can I use my pillar 3a pension for a mortgage?

Yes, you can pledge your pillar 3a pension savings as collateral for a mortgage, which can help you reach the 20% equity requirement. Some lenders also allow early withdrawal of pillar 3a funds for home purchase, subject to tax implications. This is a common strategy for first-time buyers in Switzerland.

What are the upfront costs when buying in Zurich?

Upfront costs typically range from 3% to 5% of the purchase price. They include property transfer tax (1‑2%), notary and land register fees (0.5‑1%), appraisal fee (CHF 1,000‑3,000), and mortgage setup fee (0.5‑1% of the loan). These must be paid from your equity—they cannot be included in the mortgage.
