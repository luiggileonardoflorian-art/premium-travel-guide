Why Austin’s Mortgage Market is Unique

Austin has evolved from a quiet college town into a major tech hub—now nicknamed “Silicon Hills.” This transformation has brought explosive home price appreciation, an influx of high‑income tech workers, and a surge in jumbo loan demand. Yet Texas’s property tax structure, homestead protections, and lack of state income tax create a borrowing environment unlike any other in the U.S.

1. Current Market Dynamics

As of early 2026, Austin’s housing market is showing signs of stabilization after the post‑pandemic surge. Median home prices in Travis County hover around $675,000, while in desirable neighborhoods like Westlake Hills, Tarrytown, and Barton Creek, prices frequently exceed $2 million. Inventory has increased modestly, giving buyers slightly more negotiating power, but bidding wars are still common for well‑priced homes in top school districts.

Mortgage rates for a 30‑year fixed conventional loan are currently averaging 6.5%‑6.9% for borrowers with excellent credit (740+ FICO) and a 20% down payment. Jumbo loans—those above the conforming limit of $806,500—are seeing aggressive competition from private banks and credit unions, with rates often 0.125%‑0.25% lower than conventional jumbo products in other states.

2. Jumbo Loans & Tech Wealth Strategies

Austin’s growth as a tech hub means many buyers are not traditional W‑2 earners—they’re founders with concentrated wealth, executives with large stock option packages, or early employees holding pre‑IPO equity. These profiles require specialized underwriting.

Asset‑based lending is increasingly popular. Lenders like Frost Bank, First United, and local credit unions offer programs that use liquid assets (securities, vested stock options) to qualify for a mortgage, often without requiring two years of self‑employment history. This is crucial for founders who have recently sold a company or taken a liquidity event.

Pre‑IPO mortgage planning is another area where Austin stands out. Several national lenders have dedicated “private wealth” desks that work with borrowers to structure loans around anticipated liquidity. These loans may feature interest‑only periods or flexible prepayment terms to accommodate large lump‑sum paydowns after an IPO.

3. Texas Homestead Exemption & Property Taxes

Texas has no state income tax, but it relies heavily on property taxes to fund local services. The average effective property tax rate in Travis County is approximately 1.8%, one of the highest in the nation. For a $1M home, that’s $18,000 annually—a significant carrying cost that must be factored into any mortgage decision.

The Texas homestead exemption provides relief by exempting at least $40,000 of your home’s value from school district taxes (and more for seniors or disabled homeowners). To qualify, you must occupy the home as your primary residence and file an application with the county appraisal district. While the exemption doesn’t lower your mortgage payment directly, it reduces your monthly tax escrow, improving cash flow.

“The homestead exemption is often overlooked by newcomers. Filing it can save you hundreds of dollars per month in property taxes—money that could go toward a lower rate or a larger down payment.” — Luiggi V., Senior Mortgage Analyst

4. Refinance Strategies for Austin Homeowners

With rates having moved higher over the past two years, many homeowners are wondering whether refinancing still makes sense. The answer depends on your current rate, how long you plan to stay, and whether you want to tap equity.

Rate‑and‑Term Refinance

If you purchased in 2020‑2021 when rates were 3‑4%, refinancing today at 6.5% likely doesn’t make sense. However, if you have an adjustable‑rate mortgage (ARM) that is about to reset, or if you bought with a higher rate in 2023, you might find a lower rate now. Run our refinance calculator to see your break‑even point.

Cash‑Out Refinance

Austin home equity has grown substantially. A cash‑out refinance allows you to access that equity for renovations, debt consolidation, or investment. Texas law has strict rules: you cannot exceed 80% loan‑to‑value on a cash‑out, and you must wait at least 12 months after purchasing before doing a cash‑out. These rules are designed to protect consumers but require careful planning.

5. Local Lenders & Where to Start

While national lenders operate in Austin, local institutions often have more flexibility with jumbo underwriting and a deeper understanding of neighborhood nuances. Here are a few to consider (for educational purposes; we do not endorse any specific lender):

  • Frost Bank: Strong in wealth management and asset‑based lending.
  • University Federal Credit Union (UFCU): Competitive rates for members, especially on jumbo loans.
  • First United Bank: Known for tech founder programs.
  • Velocity Credit Union: Often offers low‑cost refinance options.

Before reaching out, gather your documentation: two years of tax returns, recent pay stubs, bank statements, and if self‑employed, a profit‑loss statement. Having these ready will streamline the process.

Frequently Asked Questions (Austin)

What is the jumbo loan limit in Austin for 2026?

In Travis County (Austin), the conforming loan limit for 2026 is $806,500 for a single‑family home. Loans above this amount are considered jumbo and typically require larger down payments (20‑30%) and stronger credit scores.

How does the Texas homestead exemption affect my mortgage?

The Texas homestead exemption reduces your property tax burden by exempting a portion of your home’s value from school district taxes. It does not directly change your mortgage rate, but lower taxes mean lower monthly escrow payments, improving affordability.

What are typical closing costs in Austin?

Closing costs in Austin typically range from 2% to 5% of the loan amount. This includes lender fees (origination, underwriting), third‑party fees (appraisal, title, survey), and prepaids (property taxes, homeowners insurance).

Can I use stock options to qualify for a mortgage in Austin?

Yes, many lenders in Austin offer asset‑based or “bank statement” loans that consider vested stock options and RSUs as qualifying income. You’ll need to provide documentation of the options and likely have a CPA verify the value. This is especially common for tech employees and founders.
