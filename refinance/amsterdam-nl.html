The Canal City

Amsterdam is the financial and cultural heart of the Netherlands, home to major multinationals, a thriving tech scene, and a massive expat community. The mortgage market here is shaped by Dutch income structures, the unique hypotheekrenteaftrek (mortgage interest deduction), and a strong rental sector. Lenders are accustomed to expat packages, self‑employed freelancers (zzp’ers), and high‑net‑worth individuals seeking canal‑belt prestige properties.

Why Amsterdam’s Mortgage Market is Internationally Focused

Amsterdam’s economy is driven by international finance, technology, and a large expat workforce. This creates a borrower base with diverse income: corporate expats with 30% ruling, self‑employed freelancers (zzp’ers), and employees of multinationals. Lenders in the Netherlands use strict loan‑to‑income criteria, but the hypotheekrenteaftrag (mortgage interest deduction) makes borrowing more affordable. The city’s tight housing market means buyers often bid above asking price, requiring pre‑approval and a clear understanding of valuation risks.

Expat & 30% Ruling

Thousands of expats work in Amsterdam’s finance and tech sectors. Many benefit from the 30% ruling, which allows a tax‑free allowance. Lenders can gross up net income by the ruling percentage, significantly increasing borrowing capacity. Some banks have dedicated expat desks.

Tech & Start‑ups

Amsterdam is a European tech hub, home to Booking.com, Adyen, and hundreds of scale‑ups. Employees often receive stock options or bonuses. Lenders typically require two years of income history, but may consider options as additional assets. Self‑employed tech contractors need to show stable earnings.

Self‑Employed (zzp’er)

The Netherlands has a large freelance workforce. Lenders usually require three years of financial statements (jaarrekeningen) and use an average of recent years’ profit. Some accept one year if the business is well‑established. A solvency buffer of 30‑40% is often expected.

“Amsterdam’s mortgage market is as international as its population – lenders are used to 30% rulings, expat packages, and freelance income, but documentation must be impeccable.”

Amsterdam Luxury Enclaves & Financing Nuances

  • Canal Belt (Grachtengordel): €1.2M–€3M+, historic canal houses. Often require renovation, and may be designated monuments – renovation loans and special insurance may be needed. Lenders may require 30% deposits.
  • Oud-Zuid (Old South): €1M–€2.5M, elegant apartments and townhouses close to Vondelpark. Popular with professionals and families. High demand, often selling above asking price.
  • The Pijp / Jordaan: €700k–€1.5M, trendy neighbourhoods with lively streets. Many apartments are small – check minimum size requirements for mortgages.

Hypotheekrenteaftrek (Mortgage Interest Deduction)

The Dutch mortgage interest deduction is one of the most generous in Europe. For owner‑occupiers, interest paid on a qualifying mortgage can be deducted from income tax for up to 30 years. The maximum tax relief rate in 2026 is estimated at 37.07%. This significantly lowers net monthly costs, making higher loans affordable. The deduction applies only to annuity or linear repayment mortgages.

Strategic insight: To maximise the deduction, choose a mortgage with a longer fixed‑rate period and ensure you meet the strict repayment requirements.

Transfer Tax & Exemptions

The standard transfer tax (overdrachtsbelasting) is 8% for investment properties. For owner‑occupiers under 35 buying a home up to €440,000 (2026 limit), the rate is 0% – a full exemption. If you are over 35 or the price exceeds the limit, you pay 2% (owner‑occupied) or 8% (investor). This tax is calculated on the purchase price and paid at notary transfer.

Amsterdam Frequently Asked Questions

How does the 30% ruling affect my mortgage application?
If you have the 30% ruling, you can apply to have your gross income increased by a factor (typically 1.3) for mortgage qualification, because a portion of your salary is tax‑free. Lenders will require an official statement from the Dutch tax authorities (Beschikking 30% ruling). This can significantly boost your borrowing capacity.
Can I get a mortgage as a freelancer (zzp’er)?
Yes, but you typically need three years of financial statements. Lenders will use an average of your profit over the last three years, sometimes excluding the most recent if it was lower. Some lenders accept one year if you have a strong track record and a larger down payment. A solvency ratio of 30‑40% is often expected.
What is NHG and should I use it?
NHG (National Mortgage Guarantee) is a government scheme that protects the lender and borrower if they cannot meet payments due to unforeseen circumstances (divorce, unemployment, etc.). It lowers the interest rate because the risk is reduced. NHG is available for properties up to a certain limit (approx. €400,000 in 2026). It’s highly recommended for first‑time buyers, but not available for investment properties.
What are the costs of buying a home in Amsterdam?
Buyer’s costs include: transfer tax (0% or 2% or 8%), notary fees (approx. €1,000‑€1,500), valuation report (€500‑€800), and possibly a mortgage advice fee. If you use a buyer’s agent (aankoopmakelaar), their fee is around 1‑2% of the purchase price. NHG comes with a one‑time fee (about 1% of the loan). Overall, budget 3‑6% of the purchase price in additional costs.
