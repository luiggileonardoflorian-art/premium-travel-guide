Why The Hague's Mortgage Market is Unique

The Hague (Den Haag) is the seat of the Dutch government and home to numerous international courts and organisations, including the International Court of Justice and the International Criminal Court. This gives the city a unique character—a blend of Dutch civil servants, international diplomats, and expatriates from around the world. The housing market reflects this diversity, with strong demand for both traditional Dutch homes and modern apartments suitable for international residents. The Dutch mortgage system, with its 100% loan-to-value allowances, NHG guarantee scheme, and full tax deductibility of mortgage interest, creates a distinctive financing environment.

1. Current Market Dynamics

As of early 2026, The Hague's housing market remains competitive but slightly cooler than Amsterdam. The average purchase price for an existing home in The Hague is approximately €475,000, with significant variation by neighbourhood. Popular areas like Archipelbuurt, Statenkwartier, and Belgisch Park command premiums (€600,000‑€1,000,000+), while more affordable options exist in Escamp and Laak (€300,000‑€400,000).

Dutch mortgage rates are typically fixed for periods of 1 to 30 years, with 10‑year and 20‑year fixed rates being the most common. As of March 2026, 10‑year fixed rates average 3.6%‑4.0% for loans with NHG guarantee, and 3.8%‑4.2% without NHG. Longer fixed periods (20‑30 years) carry slightly higher rates but offer payment security. Variable rates are available but less popular due to the tax benefits of fixed rates.

2. Dutch Mortgage Basics

The Dutch mortgage market has several unique features that borrowers must understand:

  • 100% loan‑to‑value (LTV): In the Netherlands, you can borrow up to 100% of the home's value—no down payment required. However, you'll still need to cover transfer tax (2% for existing homes, 0% for new builds), notary fees, and advisor costs (typically 4‑6% of the purchase price).
  • Mortgage interest deduction (hypotheekrenteaftrek): One of the most generous in the world—you can deduct all mortgage interest paid from your income tax for up to 30 years, provided the mortgage is fully amortizing (linear or annuity) within that period. This significantly reduces your net monthly cost.
  • Interest‑only vs. repayment: Traditional Dutch mortgages often combined interest‑only and repayment portions, but new rules require all mortgages originated after 2013 to be fully amortizing (linear or annuity) to qualify for interest deduction.
  • National Mortgage Guarantee (NHG): A government‑backed scheme that protects both borrower and lender. If you meet the conditions (purchase price ≤ €450,000 in 2026, or higher for energy‑efficient homes), you pay a one‑time fee (0.6%) and get lower interest rates plus a safety net if you can't meet payments.

3. NHG Mortgage Guarantee

The NHG (Nationale Hypotheek Garantie) is a Dutch scheme that acts as a safety net. For 2026, the maximum property value eligible for NHG is €450,000 (or €477,000 if you're making energy‑saving improvements). If you meet this threshold, you pay a one‑time premium of 0.6% of the mortgage amount (included in your loan).

Benefits of NHG include:

  • Lower interest rates (typically 0.1‑0.3% below non‑NHG rates).
  • If you're forced to sell your home at a loss due to divorce, job loss, or disability, NHG may cover the remaining debt.
  • Lenders are more willing to lend at 100% LTV with NHG.

For homes above the NHG limit, you can still get a mortgage, but you won't have this safety net, and rates may be slightly higher.

4. Expat & International Buyer Financing

Given The Hague's large international community, many Dutch lenders offer mortgages specifically designed for expats and foreign buyers. Key considerations:

  • Residence status: Most lenders require a valid residence permit (or EU citizenship) and proof of employment in the Netherlands. Some may accept a 30% ruling as part of income.
  • Loan‑to‑value: Expats can typically borrow up to 100% LTV, though some lenders may limit to 95% for those with less than 3 years in the country.
  • Income verification: You'll need employment contracts, recent pay slips, and sometimes a tax return from your home country. Lenders will assess your Dutch income and may also consider a portion of your 30% ruling benefit.
  • Currency risk: If your income is in a foreign currency, lenders will apply a discount (typically 20‑30%) to account for exchange rate fluctuations.

It's advisable to work with an independent mortgage advisor (adviseur) who specialises in expat mortgages—they know which lenders are most flexible.

5. Purchase Costs & Taxes

When buying a home in The Hague, you'll need to budget for several upfront costs beyond the purchase price:

  • Transfer tax (overdrachtsbelasting): 2% of the purchase price for existing homes. First‑time buyers under 35 may be exempt for homes up to €440,000 (as of 2026). New builds are exempt.
  • Notary fees (notariskosten): €1,500‑€2,500 for the purchase deed and mortgage deed.
  • Appraisal fee (taxatiekosten): €600‑€1,000—required by the lender to confirm the home's value.
  • Mortgage advisor fee (advieskosten): €2,000‑€4,000 if you use an independent advisor.
  • NHG premium (if applicable): 0.6% of the mortgage amount.
  • Building inspection (bouwkundige keuring): €400‑€700 (optional but recommended).

Total upfront costs typically range from 4% to 6% of the purchase price. These cannot be included in your mortgage—you must pay them from savings.

6. Refinance Strategies for The Hague Homeowners

Refinancing in the Netherlands is less common than in the U.S., but it can make sense in certain situations:

  • Rate switch at end of fixed period: Most Dutch mortgages have a fixed‑rate period (e.g., 10 years). At the end, you can switch to a new rate with your current lender (often with no penalty) or shop around and transfer the mortgage to a new lender (which may involve penalties and new advisor fees).
  • Breaking a fixed rate early: If you want to refinance before your fixed period ends, you'll likely pay a penalty equal to the interest rate difference over the remaining term. This can be substantial, so calculate carefully.
  • Home equity release (overwaarde): If your home has increased in value, you may be able to increase your mortgage to access equity for renovations or other purposes. This is subject to the same affordability tests and LTV limits (max 100% of current value).
  • Switching from interest‑only to repayment: If you have an older interest‑only mortgage, you might consider refinancing to a repayment mortgage to ensure full deductibility and build equity.

Always consult a mortgage advisor to compare the costs and benefits of refinancing.

7. Local Lenders & Where to Start

The Dutch mortgage market is dominated by a few large banks, but there are also independent lenders and advisors. For educational purposes, here are some lender types to consider (we do not endorse any specific institution):

  • Major banks: ABN AMRO, ING, Rabobank, and BNG Bank offer a full range of mortgage products and have expat desks.
  • Specialist lenders: NIBC, MUNT Hypotheken, and Florius often have competitive rates and may be more flexible for non‑standard situations.
  • Independent mortgage advisors (adviseurs): In the Netherlands, most people use an advisor to shop multiple lenders. Look for advisors registered with the AFM (Authority for Financial Markets) and with experience in expat mortgages if applicable.
  • Online comparators: Websites like Independer and Hypotheker can give you an initial overview of rates.

Before applying, gather your documentation: employment contract, recent pay slips, tax returns (or jaaropgaven), passport/ID, residence permit, and bank statements. For expats, also have proof of 30% ruling if applicable.

Frequently Asked Questions (The Hague)

What is the maximum mortgage I can get in The Hague?

In the Netherlands, your maximum mortgage is generally based on your income and the property value. You can typically borrow up to 100% of the home's value (no down payment required), but strict affordability tests apply. For 2026, the maximum loan-to-value for a new mortgage is 100%.

How does the NHG mortgage guarantee work?

NHG (National Mortgage Guarantee) is a Dutch scheme that protects both you and the lender if you can't meet your payments. For 2026, the NHG limit is €450,000 (or higher for energy-efficient homes). It offers lower interest rates and a safety net, but comes with a one-time fee of 0.6% of the mortgage amount.

Can expats get a mortgage in The Hague?

Yes, many Dutch lenders offer mortgages to expats and international buyers. Requirements typically include a valid residence permit, employment contract, and proof of income. Some lenders may accept 30% ruling benefits as income. Loan-to-value limits may be slightly lower (95-100% depending on the lender).

What are the upfront costs when buying in The Hague?

Upfront costs typically range from 4% to 6% of the purchase price. They include transfer tax (2% for existing homes), notary fees (€1,500‑€2,500), appraisal fee (€600‑€1,000), mortgage advisor fee (€2,000‑€4,000), and NHG premium if applicable (0.6%). First‑time buyers under 35 may be exempt from transfer tax for homes up to €440,000.
